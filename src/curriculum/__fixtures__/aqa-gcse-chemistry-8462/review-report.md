## Misconceptions, Task Rules, and Source Mappings
Misconception rules (12 rules)
- 4.1.1.3 The development of the model of the atom (common content with physics) | severity 2 | Treats the plum pudding model as a ball of positive energy or places electrons outside the positive sphere instead of embedded within it.
  triggers: plum pudding model is a ball of positive energy; electrons orbit outside the positive ball; positive sphere with no electrons named
  correction: In the plum pudding model the atom is a sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded within it; it is an early model, not the modern nuclear atom.
- 4.2.2.5 Polymers | severity 3 | Treats poly(ethene) as a giant structure that melts by breaking covalent bonds rather than by overcoming intermolecular forces between molecules.
  triggers: polymer is a giant covalent structure so covalent bonds must break to melt; boiling point depends on breaking the carbon chain bonds; intermolecular forces and covalent bonds are the same thing
  correction: For simple hydrocarbons and polymers, boiling and melting depend on the strength of intermolecular forces between molecules, not on breaking the covalent bonds within each molecule.
- 4.4.2.3 Soluble salts | severity 3 | Describes salt preparation as evaporating the acid away or skips the excess-solid and filtration steps needed before crystallisation.
  triggers: evaporate the acid; heat to dryness to get crystals; crystallise without filtering excess solid
  correction: For a soluble salt from an insoluble base or carbonate, add the solid in excess to the acid, filter off the excess solid, then gently evaporate water so crystals form on cooling.
- 4.4.2.5 Titrations (chemistry only) | severity 2 | Gets the titration endpoint direction wrong when acid is added to alkali or describes the endpoint as clear instead of colourless.
  triggers: phenolphthalein turns pink at the end when acid is added; solution goes clear; add alkali until the indicator changes
  correction: When acid is added from the burette to an alkali with phenolphthalein, the endpoint is the change from pink to colourless; the colourless endpoint still depends on using the correct direction of addition.
- 4.4.2.6 Strong and weak acids (HT only) | severity 3 | Defines a weak acid by a low pH instead of by partial ionisation or dissociation in aqueous solution.
  triggers: weak acid means low pH; weak acid has few hydrogen ions because it is dilute; weak acid partially ionises other substances
  correction: A weak acid is one that only partially ionises in water. Strength is about extent of ionisation, not simply the pH number or whether the solution is dilute.
- 4.4.3.4 Electrolysis of aqueous solutions | severity 3 | Explains oxygen production in aqueous electrolysis using oxide or oxygen ions rather than hydroxide ions and misuses the idea of discharge.
  triggers: oxide ions make oxygen in water electrolysis; oxygen is discharged at the electrode; oxygen ions move to the positive electrode
  correction: In aqueous electrolysis, hydroxide ions are formed from water and are oxidised at the positive electrode to produce oxygen; it is the ions that are discharged, not the oxygen gas.
- 4.5.1.1 Energy transfer during exothermic and endothermic reactions | severity 3 | Explains exothermic change by saying matter or gas is lost, or mentions only bond making or only bond breaking.
  triggers: exothermic means matter is lost; gas takes the energy away; energy only released when bonds are made with no bond-breaking comparison
  correction: Exothermic reactions release more energy when new bonds form than they require to break existing bonds; the change is about energy transfer, not loss of matter.
- 4.6.2.3 Equilibrium | severity 3 | Claims that a catalyst shifts equilibrium to the products or ignores that equal total moles on both sides mean pressure does not change the equilibrium position.
  triggers: catalyst shifts equilibrium to the right; pressure always moves equilibrium to the side with fewer moles even when the totals are equal; faster rate means higher equilibrium yield
  correction: A catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally and does not change equilibrium position. Pressure only changes equilibrium when the total gaseous moles differ between sides.
- 4.7.3.4 DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) and other naturally occurring polymers | severity 2 | Answers DNA-building-unit questions with bases or the names of bases instead of nucleotides.
  triggers: dna is made of bases; adenine thymine cytosine guanine are the repeating units; dna is made from amino acids
  correction: DNA is a polymer of nucleotides. Each nucleotide includes a sugar, a phosphate group, and one base, so bases alone are not the repeating unit.
- 4.8.3.7 Flame emission spectroscopy | severity 2 | Uses the abbreviation FES or describes flame emission spectroscopy as more precise rather than naming it fully and explaining its greater accuracy.
  triggers: use fes; flame emission is more precise; instrumental method gives more ions at once so that is the main advantage
  correction: The full method name is flame emission spectroscopy. Its key advantage over a flame test is greater accuracy and more reliable measurement of the ions present.
- 4.9.2.2 Human activities which contribute to an increase in greenhouse gases in the atmosphere | severity 3 | Explains rising atmospheric carbon dioxide or methane by human respiration alone or omits the causal link from demand to fuel burning or cattle farming.
  triggers: more people respire so carbon dioxide rises; more cattle means more methane with no food-demand link; population increase causes greenhouse gases without any activity named
  correction: Population growth raises demand for transport, electricity, heating, and food, so more fossil fuels are burned and more cattle farming occurs, increasing carbon dioxide and methane emissions.

  triggers: bioleaching uses plants; bioleaching is the same as phytomining; bacteria directly make pure copper
  correction: Bioleaching uses bacteria to produce leachate solutions containing dissolved copper compounds or ions; phytomining uses plants to take up metal compounds from the soil.
